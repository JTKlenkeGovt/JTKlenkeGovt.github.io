Choosing the Poverty Line

One of the most important things that the US Census Bureau does every year is setting the federal poverty line (FPL). This number is used by dozens of government agencies and thousands of non-governmental organizations (like your local homeless shelter or food bank) to determine how to distribute resources. The metric also provides a value to track over time and see how policies or other factors effect poverty over time. Ultimately, this number is used to count the number of people in poverty.

But remember just because someone showed you a number doesn't mean it tells the whole story. The federal poverty line is ultimately arbitrary and doesn't take into account important factors like geographic location, cost of living and more. There are other measures of poverty that consider those factors (like the Cost of Living Index or Purchasing Power Parity) but for now we'll keep things simple and just look at the FPL for single individuals. This is worthwhile because lots of federal polices tie benefits to an individuals income as a percentage of the FPL, examples include Children's Health Insurance Program, the National School Lunch Program and Medicaid.

Alright, now that we know a bit about the FPL and its potential effects. Let's try to set it ourselves! At least on paper. Remember all those government agencies and non-governmental organizations that rely on this information? Unfortunately, the metric is now useless. Maybe try a different value?

This is Matthew Davis, he is a 61 year old disabled Army veteran. While he does receive veterans benefits, he is unable to work full time and has an annual income of just over $6,000. Under the Total Disability Individual Unemployability (TDIU) rating, disabled veterans are limited in the amount of paid work they are allowed to do. Getting a job while you’re on TDIU can be grounds for the Veterans Affairs Services (VAS) to revoke your benefits. VAS permits veterans to earn up to the federal poverty line for one person. Unfortunately, now that value is so low that Matthew is must either stop working or loose his benefits. This gives him no choice but to end his employment.

Because of this lowered poverty line, Matthew will lose thousands of dollars of wages and be unable to contribute to his community with paid work. The lower poverty line has made him more reliant on government services and prevented him from contributing to the workforce in any capacity.

Is it fair for the federal poverty line to be below $5000 a year? The USDA's lowest-cost plan for food is almost $3,000 a year and the national average amount spent on food is more than $5,000. Does it really seem reasonable that spending about 60% of your income on food doesn't put you in poverty? What about housing?

This is Lauren Brown, she is a 34 year old single mother living in who is pregnant with her second daughter. She works at Costco and makes about $20 an hour. She works nearly full time and struggles to pay for child care. She relies on Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) to ensure she and her family have enough to eat. The cutoff for eligibility is 130% of the federal poverty line. Two year ago, when she was making $18 an hour, she qualified for the program. But now that she makes more than $20,000 a year, she no longer qualifies for the program. That means that even though Lauren is struggling to pay rent and feed her family, she no longer receives SNAP benefits.

The problems that Lauren faces are widespread among low and middle income Americans. Many would consider $20+ an hour to be a good wage, and comparatively speaking, it is. However, despite getting a higher paying job Lauren finds herself worse off than she was before because she lost so much in benefits. This is what many people call the "wellfare trap" where people are incentivised to stay on welfare programs because earning more money would mean they no longer qualify for benefits and worse off than before.

Is setting a federal poverty line and tieing benefits to this number strengthening this trap? Is it possible that the very existence of this number to measure poverty is making it worse?

This is Nick Thompson, he is 28 years old and works part time as a graphic designer making around $25,000 a year. However, his parents are millionaires who often cover his rent or finance his expensive hobbies.

Does he deserve to be categorized as a person in poverty? He has essentially no risk of becoming homeless or not being able to afford food. Is income too 1-dimensional to measure something as complex as poverty? Maybe deciding on one single number to encapsulate poverty is an impossible task. But is it useful?

This is Thomas Horton and his wife, Pamela Mudge they live near Pittsburgh, they make a little more than $40,000 a year. Mr. Horton, 38, and a teenage son receive disability benefits, and Ms. Mudge works part time so she can assist with his care for a bone disease that has required several back operations. However, despite being near the median income for working adults and comfortably above the poverty line. Their family still struggles to provide even basic luxuries to their kids.

Is this family living in poverty? If they are living in a situation where they cannot afford even a single vacation or clothes that keep their kids from being teased at school are they doing well?

As you've chosen it, the federal poverty line is set above the median income for working single Americans. Are more than half of working Americans living in poverty? Maybe, but if so, it doesn't seem like a particularly useful metric. If we want to classify those living in poverty as especially deserving of aid, then labeling more than half of people isn't particularly useful, which is the ultimate goal of deciding on a poverty line.
