One of the most important decisions in the process of policy making is deciding how to measure the effects of the policy. This might at first seem like an innocuous task. While it might be hard to collect good data, once its collected we'll have all our answers and know exactly what is happening with the thing we're measuring. Right?

On the other hand, we know that scrupulous politicians are prone to lie and mislead with statistics. Just look at this famously misleading graph or this bus. But even in the best of circumstances, with the best intentions, counting things can be hard. Deborah Stone, a pioneer of political science, argues that the act of counting and the numbers they produce are an inherently political. This means all the messiness and disagreement inherent to politics apply to numbers as well.

You will see that through the act of counting, there are thousands of little decisions, dials that are turned, sliders that are moved, that create downstream effects. You will see how the manipulation of numbers is the norm, not the exception. To gain an understanding of numbers, explore this website and see how moving the hidden sliders behind the policy process can affect your life.

Numbers Categorize

One of the unseen effects of counting is to label and categorize. To perform even the most simple data collection, people, events, identities and problems have to be categorized.

Numbers Normalize

Through the act of counting, we begin to normalize a problem or state of the world. "Measuring any phenomenon inherently creates norms about how much is too little, too much or just right." -Deborah Stone, Policy Paradox

Numbers Simplify

Numbers simplify the complexities of the real world into just a few or even just one number that must necessarily lack nuance.

Numbers Lie

Through their inherent ambiguity numbers can lie and mislead. Numbers are constantly part of a political struggle to explain an phenomenon or provide an interpretation.
